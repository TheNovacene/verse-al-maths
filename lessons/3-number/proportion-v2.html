Understanding Proportion: Direct, Inverse & Scaling

GCSE Maths | Pearson Edexcel (Higher) | 40–60 mins | Neurodivergent-Friendly

🧠 The Knowing World

Proportion lets us compare two ratios: direct (increase/increase) or inverse (one increases, the other decreases).

Key ideas & rules

  • Direct proportion: two quantities increase together. If \(y ∝ x\), then \( y = kx \).
  • Inverse proportion: one increases while the other decreases. If \(y ∝ \tfrac{1}{x}\), then \( y = \tfrac{k}{x} \).
  • Constant of proportionality (\(k\)): the multiplier in direct/inverse formulas.
  • Scaling / similar shapes: side lengths scale in proportion, areas scale by square, volumes by cube.
  • Graphs: direct proportion → line through origin; inverse proportion → curve hyperbola.
  • Compound proportion: chaining proportional changes (e.g. speed, time, cost).

Quick check

  1. If \(y = 3x\) and \(x = 5\), what is \(y\)?
  2. If \(x\) doubles in direct proportion, what happens to \(y\)?
  3. In inverse proportion, if \(x\) doubles, what happens to \(y\)?
  4. A model car is 1/25 of real size. If a real building is 100 m high, how tall is the model?
🌍 The Human Story

Proportion is everywhere: scaling recipes, maps, population densities, speed/time, economics. It helps us make fair trades, figure out dosage, understand scale in design.

Justice prompt: Suppose a textbook uses a map that shrinks a region disproportionately, exaggerating distances for some areas. What biases or misunderstandings could that introduce? Proportion ensures fairness in representation.
