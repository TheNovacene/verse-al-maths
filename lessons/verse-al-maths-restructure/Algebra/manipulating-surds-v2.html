Surds are irrational square roots that can't be simplified into a whole or fractional number. But we can still work with them using mathematical rules.

What are Surds?

  • Surds are roots that cannot be simplified to rational numbers — e.g., √2, √3, √5.
  • Simplifying: √50 = √(25 × 2) = 5√2
  • Adding/Subtracting: You can only combine like terms, e.g., 3√2 + 2√2 = 5√2
  • Multiplying: Use the rule √a × √b = √(ab), e.g., √2 × √3 = √6

Key Tip:

Think of surds like algebra: treat √2 as an unknown symbol that only combines with other √2s unless simplified first.

üåç The Human Story

Surds emerged from a need to measure lengths that couldn’t be written exactly, like diagonals or irrational areas. Ancient mathematicians like the Greeks discovered √2 when exploring geometry — it changed their view of number forever.
