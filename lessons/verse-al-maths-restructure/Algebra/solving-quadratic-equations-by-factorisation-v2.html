üß† The Knowing World

We begin by asking: given a quadratic like \(x^2 + 5x + 6 = 0\), how do we find the values of \(x\)? Can factorisation help us “undo” the equation?

Exploration prompts

  • Start with simple quadratics (monic): \(x^2 + 5x + 6 = 0\), \(x^2 + 7x + 10 = 0\).
  • Factor the left side: \(x^2 + 5x + 6 = (x + 2)(x + 3)\).
  • What does setting \((x + 2)(x + 3) = 0\) tell you about \(x\)? (Zero product rule → one factor must be zero.)
  • Generalise: if \((x + m)(x + n) = 0\), then \(x = -m\) or \(x = -n\).

Formal steps

  1. Write the quadratic in standard form: \(ax^2 + bx + c = 0\).
  2. Factor the quadratic into two bracketed expressions.
  3. Apply the zero product rule: set each factor = 0.
  4. Solve each resulting linear equation for \(x\).

Quick check

  • Solve \(x^2 + 5x + 6 = 0\).
  • Solve \(x^2 - 3x + 2 = 0\).
  • Does \(x^2 + 4x + 5 = 0\) have real integer solutions? Why or why not?
üåç The Human Story

Factorisation of equations has roots (no pun intended) in the history of algebra. For centuries, mathematicians solved geometric problems by translating them into equations. The technique of breaking down expressions to find unknown solutions empowered leaps in science, engineering, and economics.
