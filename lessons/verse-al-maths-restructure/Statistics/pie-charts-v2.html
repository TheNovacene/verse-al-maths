Driving Question: How can a circle show whether the world is being shared fairly?

üß† The Knowing World

Focus Concept (KS4 Statistics): Using pie charts to represent categorical data as fractions, percentages and angles, especially in Sustainable Development Goal (SDG) contexts.

Seeds of Change (KS4): Interpret and construct pie charts; use fractions, percentages and proportional reasoning to describe and compare sectors.

Activity 1 – Pie Chart Builder (SDG Data & Angles)

Use this app to practise turning SDG-themed category data into fractions, percentages and angles, and then into a pie chart.

Copy of Pie Chart Builder Web App for SDG Data (GCSE Maths) by Gerry Docherty

Key language:

  • Category – one “slice” of the data (e.g. transport, energy, food).
  • Frequency – how many times that category appears (or how many responses).
  • Fraction of total – frequency ÷ total.
  • Percentage – fraction × 100.
  • Angle – fraction × 360°, the size of the slice in the pie chart.

Using Activity 1:

  1. Choose an SDG context (e.g. household waste types, energy sources, food groups in a lunch).
  2. Look at the table of categories and frequencies.
  3. Check how the app calculates:
    • Fractions and percentages for each category.
    • Angles for each slice (angle = fraction × 360°).
  4. Observe how the pie chart changes when you edit the frequencies.
  5. Try the “My SDG Scenario” option and build a pie chart for your own SDG-related data (e.g. “How my family uses water in a day”).

üí¨ Prompt: In your own words, explain why each slice of the pie chart is like a “share of 360°” and a “share of 100%”.

Activity 2 – Read the Circle (SDG Pie Chart Explorer)

Now practise reading and interpreting pie charts about the SDGs.

Using Activity 2:

  1. Choose an SDG scenario (e.g. emissions by sector, time spent on subjects, after-school activities).
  2. Look at the pie chart and legend:
    • Which slice is largest? Which is smallest?
    • Which slices together make about half of the circle?
  3. Answer the questions in the app by estimating percentages and comparing slices.
  4. Use the “Build My Own Pie Story” mode to create your own SDG-based pie chart and one-sentence story.

üí¨ Prompt: For one chart, describe which category you think should increase or decrease if we want to move closer to the SDG goals, and why.

üåç The Human Story

Pie charts are about shares: who or what gets which portion of something limited – time, money, energy, land, attention.

In SDG contexts, a pie chart might show:

  • How a government splits its budget between health, education, transport and defence (SDG 3, 4, 10).
  • How people in a city travel: walking, cycling, public transport, car (SDG 11).
  • How electricity is generated: fossil fuels vs renewables (SDG 7 & 13).

If one slice is huge (e.g. fossil fuels) and others (e.g. renewables) are tiny, that tells a story about what is being prioritised – and who might be harmed or helped.

Activity 3 – Comparing Pie Charts (SDG Fair Share)

Use this app to compare two pie charts side-by-side in SDG scenarios, and think about fairness and change.

Copy of Comparing Pie Charts Web App for GCSE SDG Scenarios by Gerry Docherty

Using Activity 3:

  1. Select an SDG scenario (e.g. budget in Region A vs Region B, transport modes in City 1 vs City 2, energy mix in Country X vs Y).
  2. Compare the two pies:
    • Which category’s share increased from Pie A to Pie B?
    • Which category’s share decreased?
    • Do any categories stay about the same?
  3. Think about what these changes mean in real life:
    • More spending on education? Less on fossil fuels?
    • More walking and cycling? Less car use?
  4. Try the “My Fair Share Story” mode to create your own comparison (e.g. “How our school spends its budget now vs how we’d like it to be”).

üó£ The Expressive Self

üé® A pie chart is like a cake being shared. Some slices are big, some are tiny, and together they make a whole.

Choose one SDG context you’ve explored (or make your own) and do one of the following:

  • Draw a simple pie chart sketch and annotate each slice with feelings as well as numbers, for example: “Car use – 40% – noisy & polluting”, “Walking – 15% – calm & healthy”.
  • Write a short paragraph starting with: “If this pie chart was a story about our world, it would say that…”

üí¨ Prompt: How does it feel to see something you care about (like nature, health, or education) as just one slice of a pie?

üèó The Designed World

Planners and leaders can use pie charts to decide where to focus effort and resources. But we can also use pie charts to imagine better designs for the future.

Design Challenge:

  1. Choose one SDG topic (e.g. how energy is generated, how a school day is spent, how a city travels).
  2. Using Activity 1 or 3, create:
    • A “current” pie chart showing how things are now.
    • An “ideal” pie chart showing how you think things should be to support people and the planet.
  3. Write or record 3–4 bullet points explaining:
    • Which slices grew or shrank in your ideal pie.
    • How these changes could help move towards the SDGs.

‚ôø Ethical Design Prompt: When you re-design a pie chart (e.g. budget, time, energy), how can you make sure that changes really support the groups who had the smallest or most vulnerable slices before?

üåÄ The Living Body

üìç Sometimes pie charts are about very human things: time, money, food, energy. Seeing small slices for important needs can feel heavy.

Notice how your body feels when you see:

  • A huge slice for something harmful (e.g. pollution, junk food, car journeys).
  • A tiny slice for something caring (e.g. green space, rest, exercise, community time).

ü´Å Try a simple grounding exercise:

  • Imagine a circle that represents your energy for today.
  • Close your eyes (if safe) and breathe in for 4 counts, imagining that circle gently expanding.
  • Breathe out for 6 counts, imagining the circle smoothing, not breaking into stressful slices.
  • When you open your eyes, choose one small action that protects your own “rest & joy” slice today.

üí¨ Prompt: If you drew a pie chart of your own day (sleep, school, screens, movement, connection, rest), what tiny change would help your chart feel kinder to your body and mind?

üõ† Final Task: Sharing the Circle – An SDG Pie Chart Story

Your Task: Create and interpret a pie chart (or pair of pie charts) in an SDG context, and explain what they show about fairness and priorities.

Choose one pathway:

  1. Pathway A – One Pie Chart
    • Use Activity 1 (Pie Chart Builder) with either:
      • One of the preset SDG data sets, or
      • Your own “My SDG Scenario” data.
    • Record:
      • Category names and frequencies.
      • Fractions, percentages and angles for each slice.
    • Write a paragraph (6–8 sentences) explaining:
      • Which category has the largest share and what that means in context.
      • Which category has the smallest share.
      • One change you would like to see and why.
  2. Pathway B – Two Pie Charts Compared
    • Use Activity 3 (Comparing Pie Charts) to choose an SDG scenario (or build “My Fair Share Story”).
    • Record the percentages for each category in Pie A and Pie B.
    • Write a comparison paragraph explaining:
      • Which slices grew and which shrank between the two charts.
      • Whether you think the change moves closer to or further from the SDG goals.
      • Who might feel the impact of this change in real life.

What to Submit (Canvas Assignment):

  • A screenshot or sketch of your pie chart(s).
  • A table showing categories, frequencies and percentages (and angles if used).
  • Your written explanation or comparison paragraph.

Assessed using the GCSE Maths Rubric:

  • Conceptual Understanding: Do you correctly convert between frequency, fraction, percentage and angle, and interpret slices in context?
  • Fluency & Accuracy: Are your totals close to 100% / 360°, and are your calculations clearly shown?
  • Application to Problems: Do you make sensible statements about fairness, priorities or change using the pie chart(s)?
  • Independence & Reflection: Do you reflect in your own words on what the distribution suggests about how the “pie” is being shared, and how you might want it to change?
