Driving Question: How can one picture tell the story of a whole group?

üß† The Knowing World

Focus Concept (KS4 Statistics): Using the five-number summary and quartiles to build box plots and describe a distribution.

Copy of Box Plot Builder Web App for KS4 Maths by Gerry Docherty

Key language:

  • Minimum – the smallest value in the data set.
  • Lower quartile (Q1) – the value 25% of the way through the ordered data.
  • Median – the middle value (50% point).
  • Upper quartile (Q3) – the value 75% of the way through the ordered data.
  • Maximum – the largest value in the data set.
  • Interquartile range (IQR) – Q3 − Q1, a measure of the spread of the middle 50% of the data.

Using the Box Plot Builder:

  1. Enter a small data set (e.g. some test scores) into the app.
  2. Check the sorted data and the five-number summary: min, Q1, median, Q3, max.
  3. Look at the box-and-whisker plot the app draws for you.

üí¨ Prompt: Where does the box sit on the number line? Is it wide or narrow? What does that tell you about how “bunched” or “spread out” your data is?

üåç The Human Story

Box plots are used to compare groups of people: exam results between two classes, finishing times in a race, waiting times in hospitals, wages in different jobs.

Instead of looking at every single number, we use the five-number summary to get a quick picture of:

  • Where the data is centred (the median).
  • How spread out it is (IQR, range).
  • Whether one group is generally higher or lower than another.

üèó The Designed World

Now let’s use box plots to compare two different groups and make evidence-based statements.

Step 1 – Compare two groups (Activity 2)

Copy of Interactive Box Plot Comparison Web App for GCSE Maths by Gerry Docherty
  1. Choose a context (e.g. Class A vs Class B test scores, boys vs girls, two different years).
  2. Enter the two data sets into the app as Group A and Group B.
  3. Look at:
    • The median for each group.
    • The IQR for each group.
    • The overall range (max − min) for each group.
  4. Use the side-by-side box plots to compare:
    • Who generally did better (higher median)?
    • Who is more consistent (smaller IQR)?
    • Is there overlap between the boxes and whiskers?

üí° Key GCSE Idea: When comparing two distributions, comment on median (location/typical value) and IQR or range (spread). Use comparative language: “higher than…”, “more spread out than…”.

Step 2 – Buddy Challenge

üë• With a partner:

  1. Use the app to generate or input two new data sets.
  2. Each of you writes your own short comparison of the two groups using the box plots.
  3. Swap your statements and check:
    • Did they mention both median and spread?
    • Did they avoid vague words like “better” without evidence?

üåÄ The Living Body

üìç Comparing results can feel stressful, especially when you are one of the results.

  • Notice how your body feels when you see two groups compared.
  • Do you tense up? Zone out? Get competitive? Feel small?

ü´Å Try a quick reset:

  • Breathe out for 6 counts and say, “I can learn from it.”
  • Repeat a few times.
