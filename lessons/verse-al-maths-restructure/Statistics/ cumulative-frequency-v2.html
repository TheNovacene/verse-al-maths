Driving Question: How can cumulative frequency help us see who is most affected?

Focus Concept (KS4 Statistics): Using cumulative frequency to describe how data builds up and to estimate medians and quartiles from real-world (SDG) contexts.

Seeds of Change (KS4): Construct and interpret cumulative frequency tables and graphs; use them to estimate and interpret medians and quartiles.

Activity 1 – Cumulative Frequency Table Builder (SDG Data)

Start by exploring how cumulative frequency tables are built from frequency tables, using SDG-themed data sets.

Copy of Cumulative Frequency Table Builder Web App for KS4 Maths (SDG Data) by Gerry Docherty

Key language:

  • Frequency – how many times a value (or class) occurs.
  • Cumulative frequency – a running total of frequencies up to and including that value or class.
  • Total frequency (N) – the sum of all frequencies.
  • Median position – at cumulative frequency N/2.
  • Quartile positions – at N/4 (Q1) and 3N/4 (Q3).

Using Activity 1:

  1. Select an SDG context, such as:
    • SDG 3 – hours of sleep for a group of teenagers.
    • SDG 6 – litres of water used per person per day.
    • SDG 7 – daily household electricity use.
  2. Observe the frequency table and then the cumulative frequency column.
  3. Step through the cumulative frequencies if the app allows: how does each new row build on the ones before?
  4. Identify the total frequency N.

üí¨ Prompt: In your own words, describe what cumulative frequency means in one of the SDG contexts. For example: “By the time we reach 60 minutes of exercise, the cumulative frequency tells us how many teenagers do up to 60 minutes of exercise.”

Activity 2 – Cumulative Frequency Graph Explorer (SDG Contexts)

Next, explore how cumulative frequency graphs (ogives) are drawn from grouped data and how we can use them to estimate medians and quartiles.

Copy of Cumulative Frequency Graph Explorer Web App for GCSE Maths (SDG Context) by Gerry Docherty

Using Activity 2:

  1. Choose a scenario (e.g. study time per week, physical activity minutes, or commute times).
  2. Look at the grouped data and its cumulative frequency table.
  3. Watch how the app plots points (upper class boundary, cumulative frequency) and joins them to make the cumulative frequency curve.
  4. Use the tools in the app to find:
    • The median (at cumulative frequency N/2).
    • The lower quartile Q1 (at N/4).
    • The upper quartile Q3 (at 3N/4).

üí¨ Prompt: Choose one context (e.g. commute time). What does the median tell you about students’ experience? What does the interquartile range (IQR = Q3 − Q1) tell you about how varied their experiences are?

üåç The Human Story

Cumulative frequency turns lists of numbers into a picture of how many people are at or below each value.

In SDG contexts, that might mean:

  • How many people get at most 6 hours of sleep per night (SDG 3: Good Health and Well-being).
  • How many households use at most a certain amount of water per day (SDG 6: Clean Water and Sanitation).
  • How many students travel less than 20 minutes to school (SDG 11: Sustainable Cities and Communities).

When we look at the curve, we’re really asking: “By this point, how many people are included?”

‚öñÔ∏è Cumulative frequency graphs can highlight inequality: for example, if one group’s curve grows quickly at low values (short commute times) and another group’s curve lags behind, that tells a story about access, opportunity, or strain.

üó£ The Expressive Self

üé® Imagine the cumulative frequency curve as a journey:

  • At the start, almost nobody is included.
  • As we move along the x-axis (time, hours, usage), more and more people join the total.
  • Eventually, everyone is included at the top of the curve (total frequency N).

Choose one SDG scenario (sleep, water use, commute time) and do one of the following:

  • Draw a sketch of the cumulative frequency curve and label where the median and quartiles are. Add speech bubbles like: “Half of us are here by this point” or “Most of us are included by here.”
  • Write a short paragraph or poem that personifies the curve (e.g. “I climb steadily as more teenagers get enough sleep…”).

üí¨ Prompt: If you think of yourself as a point on the curve in one context (e.g. sleep time), where would you like to be, and why?

üèó The Designed World

Now we’ll use cumulative frequency graphs to compare two groups in SDG-related scenarios and make evidence-based statements.

Activity 3 – Comparing Cumulative Frequency Graphs (SDG Stories)

Copy of SDG-Themed Cumulative Frequency Graph Comparator for GCSE Maths by Gerry Docherty

Using Activity 3:

  1. Select an SDG scenario (e.g. two different communities’ water use, two groups’ commute times, or two classes’ study hours).
  2. Study the two cumulative frequency curves on the same axes (Group A vs Group B).
  3. Use the tool to estimate:
    • The median for each group.
    • The quartiles for each group (Q1, Q3).
  4. Compare the groups:
    • Which group has the higher median?
    • Which group has the larger IQR (more variation in the middle 50%)?
    • For lower values (e.g. shorter commutes, more sleep), which group has a higher cumulative frequency earlier?

üë• Buddy Challenge: With a partner, pick one SDG scenario and write a joint statement using the graph: “In this context, Group A generally experiences … while Group B tends to … . We know this because the median and quartiles show … .”

‚ôø Ethical Design Prompt: If these graphs were being shown to decision-makers (school leaders, local government, health organisations), what extra context would you want them to see so they don’t oversimplify or blame a group based only on one graph?

üåÄ The Living Body

üìç Looking at data about sleep, water use, or commute times can feel personal or unfair. You might think: “That’s not my fault,” or “I’m stuck in the high end of this graph.”

Notice how your body responds when you see yourself (or your community) in the “upper tail” or at the “lower end” of a distribution.

ü´Å Try this grounding sequence:

  • Look away from the screen and name 3 things you can see in the room.
  • Take a slow breath in for 4 counts, and out for 6 counts.
  • Gently stretch your shoulders and hands, then return to the graph.

üí¨ Prompt: How can you use data about your life (sleep, movement, travel) to support your wellbeing, rather than judge yourself?

üõ† Final Task: Who Is Most Affected?

Your Task: Use cumulative frequency to answer a real question about one SDG context, and explain your reasoning clearly.

Choose one option:

  1. Option A – One Group, Deep Dive
    • Pick one SDG context from Activity 1 or 2 (e.g. sleep hours, commute times, water use).
    • Write down:
      • The total frequency N.
      • Positions for Q1, median and Q3 (N/4, N/2, 3N/4).
      • The estimated values of Q1, median and Q3 from the table/graph.
    • In a short paragraph (6–8 sentences), answer: “What does the median tell us about this group? What does the IQR tell us about how similar or different people’s experiences are?”
  2. Option B – Two Groups Compared (SDG Story)
    • Use Activity 3 to choose a scenario comparing two groups (Group A and Group B).
    • Estimate the median and IQR for each group from the cumulative frequency curves.
    • Write a comparison paragraph explaining:
      • Which group generally has the “better” outcome in the SDG context (e.g. shorter commutes, more sleep, safer water use)?
      • Which group shows more variation in experience, and what that might mean in real life.

What to Submit (Canvas Assignment):
