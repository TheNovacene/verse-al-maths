üß† The Knowing World

We use prime factorisation to break numbers into their building blocks. From these blocks we can assemble either the Highest Common Factor (HCF) or the Lowest Common Multiple (LCM).

üìò Key ideas

  • Prime factorisation: write each number as a product of primes (e.g., 84 = 2² × 3 × 7).
  • HCF: take only the primes common to all numbers, with their lowest powers; multiply.
  • LCM: take every prime that appears, with the highest power seen; multiply.

üß™ Quick practice

Find the HCF and LCM of the following pairs (use factor trees or repeated division):

  • 36 and 60
  • 24 and 84
  • 18 and 30

Tip: When in doubt, write the prime powers in a neat row for each number – the patterns jump out!

üåç The Human Story

Factorisation has powered fair sharing for thousands of years: arranging goods in equal groups, matching cycles (like traffic lights), and synchronising schedules. Today, prime factorisation underpins digital security and efficient packaging designs aimed at SDG 12: Responsible Consumption & Production.

Prompt: Where do you see “repeat together” moments (LCM) or “split fairly” moments (HCF) in daily life?

How to use it for HCF & LCM

  1. Build a prime factor tree for each number.
  2. Write each number as a product of prime powers (e.g., 60 = 22·3·5).
  3. HCF: take the lowest power of each prime that appears in all numbers; multiply.
  4. LCM: take the highest power of each prime that appears in any number; multiply.

Practice scenarios

  • Quick factors: (12, 18), (24, 60), (84, 120)
  • Three numbers: (12, 20, 30), (15, 18, 24)
  • Context: Two warning lights flash every 12s and 18s—when do they flash together next? (Use LCM)
  • Context: Packaging 84 and 120 items into equal boxes with no waste—what is the largest box size? (Use HCF)

Tip: colour-code primes or jot prime powers in a small table to see lowest vs highest exponents at a glance.
