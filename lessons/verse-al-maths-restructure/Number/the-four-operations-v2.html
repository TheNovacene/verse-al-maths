Driving Question: How do the four operations help us make and check real-life decisions?

Seeds of Change (Key Stage 3 – Number)

  • Use the four operations (addition, subtraction, multiplication and division) with integers and decimals.
  • Choose efficient methods (mental strategies, written methods, calculator when appropriate).
  • Use inverse operations and estimation to check answers.

Key Ideas & Vocabulary

  • Operation: a process you do to a number (add, subtract, multiply, divide).
  • Inverse: an operation that “undoes” another (addition ↔ subtraction, multiplication ↔ division).
  • Estimate: a close, sensible guess you can do in your head to check if an answer is reasonable.
  • Integer: whole number (negative, zero, positive).
  • Decimal: number that includes tenths, hundredths, etc. (e.g. 3.7, 0.45).

Quick Warm-Up (Choose 3–5)

  1. Mental addition: 47 + 38    (How did you do it in your head?)
  2. Mental subtraction: 102 − 67
  3. Mental multiplication: 6 × 24
  4. Division with remainder: 95 ÷ 4
  5. Decimals: 3.5 + 1.9,    4.2 × 3

Operations as “Moves”

Imagine a number line:

  • Addition = move right.
  • Subtraction = move left.
  • Multiplication = repeated addition (e.g. 4 × 3 = 4 + 4 + 4).
  • Division = sharing or grouping (e.g. 12 ÷ 3 = “12 shared into 3 equal groups”).

Partner Check: Inverse Operations

Work in pairs or small groups. For each calculation, write its “inverse check”.

  • 37 + 25 = 62  →   inverse: ?
  • 120 − 47 = 73  →   inverse: ?
  • 9 × 7 = 63  →   inverse: ?
  • 56 ÷ 8 = 7  →   inverse: ?

Low-Floor, High-Ceiling Practice

Start where you feel comfortable, then move up when you’re ready.

  • Level 1 – Steady Steps: Whole numbers under 100.
  • Level 2 – Stretch: Include decimals and bigger numbers.
  • Level 3 – Challenge: Mix operations and include negative numbers.

Create 2 questions at your chosen level for each operation, then solve them and check using inverses or estimation.

üåç The Human Story

Where Do We See the 4 Operations in Real Life?

Imagine a teenager planning their week:

  • They add up pocket money and part-time job earnings.
  • They subtract bus fares and snacks to see what is left.
  • They multiply to work out pay for several hours of work.
  • They divide costs fairly when sharing food or data with friends.

Fairness & Justice

Operations can help us check if things are fair:

  • Is everyone getting a fair share of group money?
  • Are “special offers” honest or a trick?
  • Is someone being underpaid for the work they do?

Discussion Prompts

  1. Think of a time when you needed to add, subtract, multiply or divide in real life (shopping, games, cooking, travel). What happened?
  2. Have you ever felt cheated because the maths wasn’t done fairly or correctly?
  3. Which operation do you trust yourself with most? Which one feels harder or less familiar?

Operations with Personality

Choose one of these ways (or invent your own) to show what the four operations are like in your mind:

  • Option A – Character Design: Turn +, −, ×, ÷ into four characters.
    • What are their personalities?
    • How do they help each other?
    • Which ones are “opposites” (inverses)?
  • Option B – Metaphor or Poem: Describe each operation using a metaphor.
    • Addition is like…
    • Subtraction is like…
    • Multiplication is like…
    • Division is like…
  • Option C – Visual Map: Create a mind map or sketch showing how the four operations connect and undo each other.

Explain Your Thinking

Under your drawing/poem/map, write 3–4 sentences:

  • Which operation feels most “friendly” to you and why?
  • Which operation feels “spiky” or difficult? What usually makes it hard?
  • How could seeing operations as characters or metaphors make problems easier to remember or understand?

Design a “Fair Deal Checker”

In this part of the lesson, you’ll use the four operations to design something useful for real-life decisions.

Step 1 – Choose a Context

Pick one (or invent your own):

  • Shopping: Comparing prices, “3 for 2” offers, discounts.
  • Food: Sharing a pizza or recipe between friends.
  • Time: Planning a study timetable or gaming time fairly in a week.
  • Money: Splitting bills, saving over several weeks.

Step 2 – Build Example Problems

Create at least 3 problems in your context. Each problem must require at least two different operations.

Example (shopping):

  • Energy drinks cost £1.20 each. You buy 3. You pay with a £5 note. How much change should you get?
  • Which operations did you use? How did you check your answer?

Step 3 – Use the Interactive Tool

Use the Canva “Deal or No Deal?” app above to:

  • Compare at least two of your own deals.
  • Check your answers from one of your written problems.
  • Notice where you are adding, subtracting, multiplying and dividing.

Step 4 – Checkers and Shortcuts

  • For each problem, write an inverse calculation or estimate to check your answer.
  • Describe any shortcuts you used (e.g. “I multiplied by 10 then halved it” for ×5).

Noticing How Maths Feels

Pause for a moment and notice:

  • How does your body feel when you see a long calculation?
  • Do your shoulders, breath or jaw change when you feel “stuck”?
  • Which operation makes you feel tense or worried?

Small Regulation Practices

  • Box Breathing: Breathe in for 4, hold for 4, out for 4, hold for 4 (repeat 3 times).
  • Shake & Reset: Gently shake out your hands, roll your shoulders, then come back.
  • Micro-break: Look away from the page/screen and find 5 objects of a certain colour in the room.

 Maths Rubric (Simplified)

  • Conceptual Understanding:
    • Do you explain what the operations mean, not just do them?
    • Have you shown how they are connected (inverse pairs, repeated addition, sharing)?
  • Fluency & Accuracy:
    • Are your calculations correct and clearly set out?
    • Have you used efficient methods (not just the first thing that came to mind)?
  • Application to Problems:
    • Does your guide/game use realistic problems (money, time, sharing, offers)?
    • Do you clearly show which operation(s) you chose and why?
  • Independence & Reflection:
    • Have you added a short reflection: what went well, what you’d change next time?
    • Did you spot and correct any mistakes using estimation or inverses?
