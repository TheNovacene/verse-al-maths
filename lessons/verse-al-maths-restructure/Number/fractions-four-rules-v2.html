The Four Rules of Fractions (Add, Subtract, Multiply, Divide)

Driving Question: How can we use the four rules of fractions to make fair and accurate decisions in real life?

GCSE Maths | Pearson Edexcel (Higher) | 40–60 mins | Neurodivergent-Friendly

üß† The Knowing World

We will learn how to add, subtract, multiply, and divide fractions with accuracy and confidence.

Key ideas

  • Equivalent fractions: multiplying or dividing top & bottom by the same non-zero number doesn’t change the value.
  • Lowest Common Denominator (LCD): to add/subtract, convert fractions so denominators match.
  • Multiplication: multiply numerators together and denominators together; simplify at the end (or cross-cancel first).
  • Division: Keep–Change–Flip — keep the first fraction, change ÷ to ×, flip the second fraction (use the reciprocal).
  • Mixed numbers: convert to improper fractions to operate; you may convert back at the end.
  • Simplify: reduce to simplest form; show factors clearly to avoid errors.

Glossary (co-construct with class)

  • Numerator: top number; how many parts.
  • Denominator: bottom number; size of each part.
  • Reciprocal: the flipped fraction (e.g., 3/5 ↔ 5/3).
  • Common denominator: a shared denominator between two or more fractions.
  • Improper fraction: numerator ≥ denominator.
  • Mixed number: whole part plus a fraction (e.g., 21/3).

Tiny worked examples

Add: 1/6 + 1/4 = (2/12) + (3/12) = 5/12.

Subtract: 3/5 − 2/15 = (9/15 − 2/15) = 7/15.

Multiply: 2/3 × 9/10 = 18/30 = 3/5 (or cross-cancel 2/3 × 9/10 → 1/3 × 9/5 → 9/15 = 3/5).

Divide: 4/7 ÷ 2/5 = 4/7 × 5/2 = 20/14 = 10/7 = 13/7.

Fractions appear in rationing, recipes, fair pay, and resource sharing. During crises, aid is often divided by fractions (e.g., “each family receives 3/4 of a parcel”). Justice prompt: If two communities receive different fractional amounts of the same resource, how can we compare and argue for fair distribution? Link to SDGs (e.g., SDG 2 Zero Hunger, SDG 10 Reduced Inequalities).

What you’ll do: Use this interactive “Fraction Rules Sandbox” to practise the four rules, model with fraction bars, and then solve a real-world design brief where fractional precision matters.

Try this

  1. Warm-up: Enter 2/3 + 3/4, then 5/12 − 1/8. Check your LCD each time.
  2. Fluency: Create three of your own × and ÷ fraction problems. Solve and simplify; show cross-cancelling if used.
  3. Reasoning: A recipe for 12 people needs 31/2 cups of flour. What fraction per person is that? If serving 8 people, what is the new amount?
  4. Ethical design prompt: Design a measuring aid for a person with one hand or low vision to portion 3/4, 1/3, and 1/8 accurately. What must change in shape, grip, markings (high-contrast, tactile), and tolerance?
  5. Buddy challenge (pairs): Swap one × and one ÷ question. Check each other’s steps; if an error is found, write a one-line “fix-it rule”.

Worked example (reasoning layout)

Problem: 11/2 ÷ 3/8

Method: Convert to improper → 3/2 ÷ 3/8 → Keep–Change–Flip → 3/2 × 8/3 → (cross-cancel 3s) → 8/2 = 4.
