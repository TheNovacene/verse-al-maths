The Number Universe: Natural → Integers → Rationals → Irrationals → Real

GCSE Maths | Pearson Edexcel (Higher) | 40–60 mins | Neurodivergent-Friendly

We will trace how different “sets of numbers” emerged, why each was needed, and how they nest together.

Key ideas & definitions

  • Natural numbers (‚Ñï): counting numbers 1, 2, 3, … (some definitions include 0).
  • Integers (‚Ñ§): the natural numbers plus zero and negatives: …, –2, –1, 0, 1, 2, …
  • Rational numbers (‚Ñö): numbers expressible as a fraction a/b, where a, b are integers and b ≠ 0.
  • Irrational numbers: real numbers that cannot be expressed as a ratio of integers (e.g. √2, π).
  • Real numbers (‚Ñù): the union of rationals and irrationals; all points on the number line.
  • Nested relationships: ‚Ñï ⊆ ‚Ñ§ ⊆ ‚Ñö ⊆ ‚Ñù, and ‚Ñù = ‚Ñö ∪ (Irrationals).
  • Density of rationals: between any two rationals is another rational.
  • Historical tensions: e.g. early Greeks couldn’t accept √2 as “number” because it’s not a ratio.

Historical journey (sketch)

– Early civilizations used natural numbers for counting, trade, and measurement. – Debts and elevation led to the need for zero and negatives → Integers. – Fractions (rationals) evolved to describe parts of wholes, trade shares, and proportions. – Greek geometry (e.g. diagonal of a square) uncovered incommensurable lengths like √2, forcing acceptance of irrationals. – Finally, the real number line unified rationals and irrationals into a continuous continuum, essential for calculus and analysis.

Quick check

  1. Is –3 a natural number? integer? rational?
  2. Write 4/7 as a rational, and explain why it’s or isn’t an integer.
  3. Why is √3 not rational? (Think about prime factorizations.)
  4. Between 1.414 and 1.415, is there a rational? name one.
üåç The Human Story

Number systems are tied to culture, philosophy, and power. Some early mathematicians regarded irrational numbers as “incomplete” or mystical. In modern times, access to advanced mathematical ideas (e.g. reals and limits) is part of educational equity.

Justice prompt: Suppose a community only teaches integers and rationals in primary schooling, leaving out irrational numbers. How might that limit students’ understanding of geometry, science, or data? What does “number literacy” mean in justice terms?
