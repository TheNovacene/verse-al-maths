Seeds of Change (Key Stage 3 – Number, Year 7)

  • Read, write and understand place value in integers (up to at least 1 000 000).
  • Understand the place value of digits in decimal numbers (tenths, hundredths, thousandths).
  • Partition numbers into expanded form and recombine them.
  • Use place value to compare, order and round numbers.

Key Ideas & Vocabulary

  • Digit: a single symbol like 0, 1, 2, …, 9.
  • Place: the position of a digit (ones, tens, hundreds, tenths, hundredths, …).
  • Value: what the digit is worth because of its place (e.g. in 4 236, the 4 is worth 4 000).
  • Place value chart: a table showing columns for each place (Th, H, T, O, . , tenths, hundredths, …).
  • Standard form (for this lesson): the usual way we write a number, e.g. 5 208.
  • Expanded form: writing a number as a sum of its parts, e.g. 5 208 = 5 000 + 200 + 8.

Warm-Up: Read, Say, Build

Teacher or slide shows a number; learners do three things:

  1. Read it out loud (in words).
  2. Say what each digit is worth.
  3. Write it in expanded form.

Suggested numbers (you can adapt):

  • 4 307
  • 50 206
  • 809 014
  • 3.7
  • 0.406

Digit of the Day

Choose a digit, e.g. 7. Ask:

  • Where could this 7 appear in 47 305? What would its value be there?
  • Where could it appear in 0.73? What is its value now?
  • What is the difference between a 7 in the tens place and a 7 in the thousands place?

Quick Practice (Low Floor, High Ceiling)

Choose your starting level, then move up when ready:

  • Level 1 – Whole Numbers: Write in expanded form:
    • 3 205, 48 012, 907.
  • Level 2 – Larger Numbers: Include thousands and hundred thousands:
    • 230 504, 1 005 030.
  • Level 3 – Decimals: Write in expanded form:
    • 4.6, 2.34, 0.507.

Check with a partner: does your expanded form recombine to the original number?

üåç The Human Story

Place Value in Everyday Life

Place value shows up in many places outside maths lessons:

  • Money: £3.75 – the 3 is worth £3, the 7 is 70p, the 5 is 5p.
  • Population & big numbers: Country populations, charity totals, views on a video.
  • Measurements: 1.75 m tall, 0.45 litres of juice, 3.06 km.
  • Time & dates: Years (2025), times (13:45), scores in games.

When Place Value Goes Wrong

Misreading place value can have real effects:

  • Thinking £0.50 is the same as £0.05.
  • Confusing 0.4 and 0.04 when measuring ingredients.
  • Misreading 10 000 as 1 000 on a form or chart.

Discussion Prompts

  1. Have you ever misread a price, score, or number because of the digits or the zeros?
  2. Why might it matter if a medicine dose or ingredient is written 0.5 instead of 0.05?
  3. Which is more: 0.4 or 0.04? How would you explain that without using a calculator?

Mini Task – Number Stories

Choose one context (money, population, sport, or something from your life). Write a short “number moment” where:

  • A character misunderstands place value (e.g. mixes up tens and ones or tenths and hundredths).
  • Someone else uses place value to spot and fix the mistake.

Highlight or underline the important digits and write a sentence explaining what each is worth.

üé® The Expressive Self

Make Place Value Make Sense to You

Choose one way (or invent your own) to express how you understand place value:

  • Option A – Place Value City: Draw a city where:
    • Each building is a place (ones house, tens tower, hundreds hotel, etc.).
    • Digits are the “residents” that move into different buildings.
    • Explain what happens when a digit moves from the ones house to the tens tower.
  • Option B – Metaphor or Poem:
    • “Place value is like…” (e.g. seats on a bus, shelves in a library, layers of a cake).
    • Write a short poem or paragraph using your metaphor to explain why the place matters.
  • Option C – Number Biography:
    • Choose a number that feels interesting to you (e.g. 3 407 or 0.508).
    • Write its “biography” – where each digit lives, what it’s worth, and what would happen if it moved places.

Explain Your Thinking

Under your drawing or writing, add 3–4 sentences:

  • Which part of place value feels clearest to you?
  • Which part feels confusing (e.g. zeros, decimals, big numbers)?
  • How does your city / metaphor / biography help you remember what digits are worth?

Using the Place Value Drag & Drop App

In this part of the lesson you will use the Canva app above to explore how numbers can be broken apart and rebuilt.

Step 1 – Get to Know the Tool

  • Click or tap to generate a target number.
  • Look carefully at each digit and think:
    • What is this digit’s place?
    • What is this digit’s value?
  • Use the value tiles to match the target number in expanded form.

Step 2 – Build and Check

  1. Choose a target number that feels manageable for you.
  2. Drag tiles into the tray to make the number in expanded form (for example 5 000 + 200 + 8).
  3. Press “Check my expanded form” in the app.
  4. If it’s not correct, adjust the tiles and try again.

Step 3 – Create Your Own Challenges

  • Pick or think of a number that:
    • Uses at least three different places, e.g. 30 405 or 0.507.
    • Includes at least one zero digit.
  • Rebuild it in the app and check it works.
  • Screenshot or copy your number and its expanded form.
  • Swap with a partner and challenge them to:
    • Rebuild your number correctly in the app.
    • Explain what each digit is worth.

Step 4 – Decimal Challenge (Optional)

If the decimal option is available in the app, or using your own examples:

  • Try numbers like 4.6, 2.34, 0.507.
  • Write them in expanded form and check using the tool or mental methods.
  • Explain in words what the digits after the decimal point mean.

Step 5 – Design Reflection

Talk or write about:

  • What did the app make easier about place value?
  • What did it make you notice that you hadn’t seen before?
  • If you could add one feature to the app to help another student, what would it be?
üß¨ The Living Body

Noticing How Place Value Feels

Place value can bring up different feelings:

  • Excited by big numbers or patterns.
  • Worried by lots of zeros or decimals.
  • Confused when digits move around.

Pause & Check-In

Take 30 seconds to notice:

  • How does your body feel when you see a long number?
  • Is your breathing fast, slow, or normal?
  • What thoughts pop up (“I can’t do this”, “This is interesting”, “I’m lost”, etc.)?

Regulation Mini-Practices

  • Number Breathing: Breathe in for 4, out for 6 while counting quietly in your head: 1, 2, 3, 4…
  • Grounding with Digits: Look around the room and find 5 numbers. Say what each digit is worth in at least one of them.
  • Kind Self-Talk: Choose a sentence you can say when stuck, e.g. “I can try one place at a time” or “It’s okay to ask for help.”

Metacognitive Reflection

Complete these sentence starters in your book or on a sticky note:

  • “I feel most confident with place value when…”
  • “Zeros / decimals feel tricky because…”
  • “One strategy that helps me is…”
