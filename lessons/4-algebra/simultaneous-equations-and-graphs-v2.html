Graphical Solutions to Simultaneous Equations

GCSE Maths | Pearson Edexcel (Higher) | 40–60 mins | Inquiry-Based | Neurodivergent-Friendly

🧠 The Knowing World

Simultaneous equations can be solved by finding where their graphs intersect. This visual method reveals the nature of solutions through slope and alignment.

Key Ideas

  • Each equation represents a straight line.
  • The point where two lines intersect is the solution to both equations.
  • No intersection → no solution (parallel lines).
  • Same line → infinite solutions.

Quick Check

  • Sketch the graphs of y = 2x + 1 and y = -x + 4. Where do they meet?
  • What does the solution represent?
  • What happens if the gradients are equal but intercepts are different?
🌍 The Human Story

Maps, economics, and engineering all rely on intersecting variables. From crossing paths to market equilibriums, humans use simultaneous equations to model real-world conditions and choices.

🎨 The Expressive Self
  • Draw or animate two lines that meet, run parallel, or overlap entirely. Use this to tell a story of conflict, agreement, or alignment.
  • Write a dialogue between two characters representing each equation. What happens when they “meet”?

Prompts to Explore

  • What happens when the gradients of both lines are equal?
  • What does this mean for the pair of simultaneous equations?
  • Can you create a pair of equations with no solution?
  • How would you adjust the equations to ensure they intersect at a specific point?
  • Try changing one gradient slowly — what do you notice about how the intersection moves?
🧘 The Living Body

Act out the equations with two people walking on intersecting paths. Where do they meet? Use string or chalk on the floor to represent their lines.

✅ Final Task

Task: Create a short explanation or visual guide for solving equations graphically. Include cases of one solution, no solution, and infinite solutions.

  • Conceptual Understanding — Use slope and intercept to describe relationships.
  • Fluency & Accuracy — Accurately plot and label graphs.
  • Application — Link graph behaviour to algebraic relationships.
  • Independence & Reflection — What kind of solutions are most useful in real life?
