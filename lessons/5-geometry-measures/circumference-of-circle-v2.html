🎯 Lesson Title: The Circumference–Diameter Relationship

GCSE Maths | Geometry | 30–50 mins | Inquiry-Based | Neurodivergent-Friendly

🧠 The Knowing World
  • The circumference is the distance around a circle.
  • The diameter is the distance across the circle through its centre.
  • If you plot circumference (C) against diameter (d), the graph forms a straight line.
  • The gradient of this line is π (pi).
  • So the formula becomes:
    C = π × d or C = 2πr (where r is the radius).

Try this:

  • Measure the diameter of a lid or cup.
  • Wrap string around the edge to find the circumference.
  • Divide circumference by diameter. What value do you keep getting?
🌍 The Human Story

For over 4,000 years, civilisations from Babylon to India to modern scientists have tried to understand circles. The number π (pi) turns up again and again. It’s not just a number—it's a bridge between art, science, and mystery.

🎨 The Expressive Self
  • Create a visual diagram showing how circumference changes as the diameter increases.
  • Record a short voice note: “What does π mean to me?”
  • Make a collage of circular objects around you, labelled with their diameter and estimated circumference.
🛠️ The Designed World

Use the interactive tool below to investigate the relationship between circumference and diameter. Resize the circle and see what happens to the graph!

Investigate:

  • Try different sizes of circle. What are the values of diameter and circumference?
  • What does the graph look like?
  • What is the gradient of the line?
  • Can you write a rule that links C and d?
🧘 The Living Body
  • Find a circular object and measure its diameter.
  • Use string to measure its circumference.
  • Make a circle motion in the air with your arms – imagine stretching it into a line!
✅ Final Task

Create a poster or slide that explains the connection between diameter and circumference. Use:

  • Your own words
  • Diagrams or photos with annotations
  • At least one real-life circular object

Reflection:

  • What surprised you about the relationship?
  • What does the gradient of your graph tell you?
  • Could you use this method to estimate π?
