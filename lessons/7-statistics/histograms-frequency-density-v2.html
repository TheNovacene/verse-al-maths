Driving Question: How can histograms show who gets more – and who gets less – in our world?

Focus Concept (KS4 Statistics): Drawing and interpreting histograms, and using frequency density so that bar area (not just height) represents frequency.

Seeds of Change (KS4): Construct and interpret diagrams for grouped continuous data, including histograms with unequal class intervals; interpret and compare distributions.

Activity 1 – Histogram & Frequency Density Explorer (SDG Contexts)

Use this app to explore how frequency, class width and frequency density work together in histograms linked to the Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs).

Copy of Histogram & Frequency Density Web App for GCSE Maths (SDG Contexts) by Gerry Docherty

Key language:

  • Class interval – a group or “bin” of values (e.g. 0–10, 10–20).
  • Class width – size of the interval: upper boundary − lower boundary.
  • Frequency – how many values fall in that class.
  • Frequency density – frequency ÷ class width.
  • In a histogram with unequal class widths, bar area ∝ frequency, not just height.

Using Activity 1:

  1. Choose an SDG context (e.g. daily water use, electricity use, commute times).
  2. Study the table of class intervals, frequencies and frequency densities.
  3. Notice how the app calculates frequency density = frequency ÷ class width.
  4. Look at the histogram: wider bars with lower height can still have the same area (and frequency) as narrow bars with higher height.
  5. Try the “My SDG Scenario” option to design your own variable and grouped data.

üí¨ Prompt: Why is it important to use frequency density rather than just frequency when class widths are different?

üåç The Human Story

Histograms don’t just show numbers – they show how a whole group’s experiences are spread out.

In SDG contexts, histograms might show:

  • How water use is spread across households in two communities (SDG 6).
  • How electricity use varies between two regions (SDG 7).
  • How commute times change before and after a new bus or cycle route (SDG 11).

If one community’s histogram has most of its area at low water use and another has a long tail of very high users, that tells a story about inequality and pressure on resources.

üó£ The Expressive Self

üé® Think of a histogram as a skyline of a city made from data.

  • Short, squat bars = lots of people with similar experiences.
  • Tall spikes = big differences or “crowds” at certain values.
  • Long tails = a few people experiencing very extreme values.

Choose one SDG context (water, energy, commute time, sleep, or something you create) and:

  • Sketch a simple histogram by hand and label it with words like “most people here”, “few people here”, “extreme cases here”.
  • Or write a mini-story starting with: “If you walked along the base of this histogram, you would see…”

üí¨ Prompt: Which feels easier for you – looking at the histogram and describing the story, or starting with a story and building a histogram to match?

üèó The Designed World

Engineers, planners and campaigners use histograms to design fairer systems:

  • Designing bus timetables based on commute time distributions.
  • Planning water supply or storage based on household usage patterns.
  • Setting sensible targets for screen time, sleep or exercise.

Design Challenge: Use what you’ve learned from the apps to imagine a small intervention in one SDG context.

  1. Pick a variable (e.g. “commute time”, “litres of water used”, “daily screen time”).
  2. Imagine a change (e.g. “what if we add more buses?”, “what if we run a water conservation campaign?”).
  3. Describe how you’d want the histogram to change:
    • Shift left (shorter times / lower use)?
    • Become more concentrated (more equal)?
    • Lose extreme tails?
  4. Optionally, build your “before and after” in Activity 3’s custom mode.

‚ôø Ethical Design Prompt: If you redesigned a system based on histograms, how would you check that changes really help the people who were in the “worst” part of the distribution, not just improve the average?

üåÄ The Living Body

üìç Seeing data about sleep, commute times, water or energy can bring up feelings like:

  • “That’s unfair.”
  • “I’m one of the unlucky ones.”
  • “I’m using too much.”

It’s okay to notice these feelings. You are not a bar on a graph.

ü´Å Try this regulation pause:

  • Look away from the screen and find 3 objects in the room that are calm or comforting.
  • Take a slow in-breath for 4 counts and imagine drawing a gentle curve.
  • Exhale for 6 counts and imagine smoothing out tension in your shoulders.
  • Roll your neck gently, unclench your jaw, and then return to the maths if you feel ready.

üõ† Final Task: Designing and Reading Histograms for Justice

Your Task: Create and interpret one or two histograms in an SDG context, and explain what they show about fairness or inequality.

Choose one pathway:

  1. Pathway A – One Histogram, Deep Dive
    • Use Activity 1 to build a histogram for one SDG data set (or your own “My SDG Scenario”).
    • Write down the grouped table with:
      • Class intervals
      • Frequencies
      • Frequency densities
    • In a paragraph (6–8 sentences), describe what the histogram shows:
      • Where most values lie.
      • Any long tails or extreme values.
      • What this might mean in the real SDG context.
  2. Pathway B – Two Histograms Compared
    • Use Activity 3 to compare two groups (e.g. Community A vs Community B, “before” vs “after” a change).
    • Record the key features of each histogram:
      • Ranges where most of the area lies.
      • Any differences in spread and extreme values.
    • Write a comparison paragraph explaining:
      • Which group tends to have “better” outcomes in context.
      • Which group shows more variability.
      • What questions this raises about fairness or access.
